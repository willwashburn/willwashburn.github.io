Every morning, after the gym, Khaliq and I take turns making omlettes for ourselves for breakfast. I usually use 4 eggs with a few egg whites, mixed with some garlic and basil and topped with some cheese and turkey (Khaliq and I have seperate, defined methods: but that’s another post for another day). By the time I finish making it, I’m starving; I haven’t eaten for a few hours of being awake, the aroma is all up in my face and I just worked out.

I never eat the omlette right away though. I always do my dishes first.

See, while the omlette is so very tempting and delicious and I am usually very hungry - it’s always in my best interest to clean the dishes right away. If I leave them it gets a lot harder to clean them later - stuff gets piled on, things harden and stick and the process gets more intensive.

Writing code is the same way, but sometimes less obvious. There are fun problems to solve and sometimes you’re so hungry to see if you’re answers work you’ll leave some dishes. You wont do the due dilligence in the beginning (like writing tests) and end up costing yourself a ton of time later on.

Just like a good cook leaves the kitchen cleaner than they left it, a good programmer sets up things the right way to start. This is something I’m just starting to realize and, unfortunately, my code has lots of dishes right now.
